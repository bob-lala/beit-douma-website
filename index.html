The house

Restored in a simple and subtle way

The oldest walls of Beit Douma were raised in the 18th century, and the house wears its age openly: the original Summaqi stone floor still runs through the library, and the Hharounn — a hollow in the floor once filled with hot coals to warm the room — has been kept where it always was.

Days here move at the village's pace. Bread comes out of the Tabouneh, the mud-brick oven on the kitchen terrace. The kitchen is open — you're welcome to cook alongside the team. The library holds books, botanical prints and a DVD shelf for slow evenings, and the terraces look over gardens and apple orchards toward four mountains.

Tawlet Beit Douma طاولة بيت دوما

The village cooks. You sit down.

Tawlet Beit Douma is Souk el Tayeb's farmers' kitchen: every Saturday and Sunday, cooks from Douma and the surrounding Batroun villages lay out an open buffet of the dishes they grew up on — mezze, saj bread, grilled lahme, taouk and makanek from the live grill, and desserts from the garden. Before the buffet, a single fixed 11:30 start: choose a guided walk through the old Douma souks, or a hands-on cooking class in the kitchen.

From the tannour & the garden

Tawlet Beit Douma is part of Souk el Tayeb — the project that started Beirut's farmers' market and built kitchens where rural women cook the food of their region, for a fair income. Eating here keeps recipes like Kebbet Douma alive, and keeps the value in the village.

The dining room spills onto the garden under green-striped awnings; in summer, lunch happens beneath the trees with the Batroun mountains as the fourth wall.

Getting here

Douma, Batroun mountains

Beit Douma sits in the heart of Douma's preserved village core — about 90 minutes from Beirut and 30 minutes up from the Batroun coast, in a valley ringed by four mountains. Come for the buffet, stay for the quiet; we're happy to help arrange transport from Beirut.
